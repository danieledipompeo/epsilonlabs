Module Descriptors

Advanced Topics in Interactive Technologies (ADVT) 2014/5

Module Code COM00021M
Lecturers adne, csp, hp, pc
Taken By ACS, HCIT, SIIT
Number of Credits 20
Terms Autumn, Spring
Teaching Autumn 2-9, Spring 2-9
Open Assessment Open Assessment [100%]
16th Feb → 15th Apr (Spr/7/Mon → Sum/1/Wed)
Feedback: 29th Apr (Sum/3/Wed)

Module Prerequisites

Prerequisite knowledge

There are no formal prerequisites for this module. It is assumed that student bring to it an open mind about human-computer interaction. Any students who have not attended UNUS, UCDE or ARMH may have to do additional reading.

Workload

  • Lectures: 2 x 1hr
  • Problem Classes: 18 x 2hrs
  • Assessment: 60hrs
  • Tutorials: 9 x 1hr

Private Study

Each lecturer will provide references which students should read in advance of the class. Part of the evaluation of the assessment is evidence that the student has read around the topic.

Students are also required to submit a summary of each class.

Assessment

Open Assessment

  • 16th Feb → 15th Apr (Spr/7/Mon → Sum/1/Wed) , uses electronic submission.
    Feedback: 29th Apr (Sum/3/Wed)

The assessment requires the writing of a paper, based on material covered in the module and read from the literature. There are three stages to the assessment. Firstly, the student must agree on a title for the paper with the lecturer. The student must then prepare an Extended Abstract of the paper. Feedback will be provided on this abstract before students start work on the assessment proper.

There is a re-assessment. It takes the same form, except that students much choose a title from a prescribed list and the Extended Abstract is not a formal requirement (i.e. it is not included in the marking).

Formative Feedback

The tutorial programme includes a group writing exercise, which is peer-assessed.

Feedback will be given on the extended abstract of the assessment paper.

Aims

The module aims to expose students to the work of leading UK interaction specialists through a series of masterclasses presented by those individuals. This gives them both a historical context for the discipline of human-computer interaction as well as exposure to a number of current research topics.

The module is assessed through the writing of an academic paper. Guidance is given on this style of writing and students are expected to demonstrate the influence of the classes and speakers they have attended.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the module students will have a good understanding of the current state of the art of human-computer interaction research and practice. They will have demonstrated this appreciation in the writing of an academic paper.

Content

Students will have 2, one-hour, lectures in the Autumn term that introduces them to reading and reviewing scientific literature. From Autumn 2 - Autumn 10 students will engage each week with readings of scientific literature from across the breadth of the HCI field. These readings will be explored and discussed in small group practicals where students compare and contrast different aspects of the literature, in particular the methodologies that were used and the implications the results in the paper have on HCI research and practice.

In Autumn 8, each student group will engage in a literature survey in depth on one of the topics covered in the reading practicals and prepare a research paper collaboratively on that topic. Each group will put their paper in an online forum. These research papers will then be peer-assessed by other groups, providing critiques and feedback about how well the literature was covered and synthesized, as well as on the presentation of the work. These peer-assessed critiques will provide formative feedback on their skills in preparing research papers.

From Spring 2 - Spring 10 students will attend seminars on a variety of advanced topics in HCI. These will be delivered by a combination of eading experts in research and high profile professionals from the field of user experience. Topics are subject to the availability of lecturers, but they will be similar to those presented in 2013-14, which were:

1. Introduction and Briefing
2. Research in Practice
3. The Web as a Personal Archive
4. Participatory Design with Older People
5. Taking Responsibility in a Computerised World
6. Dramatic interaction
7. User Experience Research Methods
8. Cross-Cultural HCI
9. POETry Interaction
10. The ChartEx Project: A Case Study in Interdisciplinary HCI
11. Are we human or are we children?
12. Games design and usability
13. Web accessibility
14. The world's smallest HCI problem
15. Multimodality

Teaching Materials

Visiting lecturers provide readings in advance which the students are expected to use in preparation. Following on, the masterclass style of teaching inevitably implies interaction and is a unique opportunity for them to get first-hand experience.

The module is supported by a VLE-style website, including an on-line Forum, feedback forms and a Twitter feed.
